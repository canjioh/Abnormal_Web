Deciding whether a number is normal takes infinitely many checks. Deciding whether it is pseudonormal takes one table.

Swap two digits throughout the base-\(b\) expansion of a real number, then subtract the result from the original. Whatever the number, only six digits can survive the subtraction — and if the number is normal, their densities are forced to the last decimal. That forced table is the \(\Delta_b\)-scheme, and this site computes it: for the constants, for your own numbers, and in every base up to sixteen.

What it computes

For a base \(b\) and two digits \(i \neq j\), the swap map \(\sigma^{i,j}_{(b)}\) exchanges \(i\) and \(j\) everywhere in the expansion, and what is left is

\[ \Delta_b^{\,i,j}(\omega) \;=\; \omega - \sigma^{i,j}_{(b)}(\omega). \]

The site builds the entire table of digit densities of those differences — one line for each of the \(b(b-1)/2\) pairs — and reads it against the line that normality forces.

The map

The permutation of the digit alphabet, and the three expansions in register: the number, its swap, and the difference. The collapse from \(b\) symbols down to six is visible on the strip itself.

The table

The scheme as a heat map. Vermilion where an entry sits above the prescribed value, indigo where it sits below, intensity by standard error — so a hundred and twenty lines can be read at a glance.

The verdict

For a rational the period is found and the densities are exact fractions, so the answer is a proof. For everything else it is a measurement on a prefix, and it is labelled as one.

Why it is worth a table

Normality is a statement about every string of every length, in every base: no finite computation settles it, and not one of the classical constants — \(\pi\), \(e\), \(\sqrt{2}\), \(\log 2\) — has ever been proved normal in a single base. Pseudonormality is what remains of normality once you insist on being able to check it.

Every normal number is pseudonormal, so a number that fails the test is not normal, full stop — and the failure is exhibited rather than asserted. The converse is false: there are rationals, normal in no base whatsoever, whose scheme is exactly the prescribed one. The test is a sieve and not a decision procedure, and a good part of the interest lies in seeing how fine a sieve it is.

The condition does become a characterisation once all bases are taken together: \(\omega\) is \(b\)-normal exactly when it is \(b^{n}\)-pseudonormal for every \(n\), and absolutely normal exactly when it is pseudonormal in every base. What is open is whether finitely many bases already suffice.

Source
The definitions, the scheme and the results implemented here are those of Normal and pseudonormal numbers, by Nicolò Cangiotti and Daniele Taufer (arXiv:2102.05925).
Honesty
Digits of the constants are produced with exact integer arithmetic at a fixed scale, with guard digits — never with floating point. Rationals are expanded until the period closes, and then the answer is exact. Everything else is reported as an estimate on a prefix, with an error bar, because that is what it is.
